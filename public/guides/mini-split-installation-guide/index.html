Mini Split Installation Guide 2026

A single-zone mini split installation is achievable for an experienced DIYer, but it has distinct steps that differ from any other home improvement project. This guide covers the full process from wall penetration to startup.

By IndustrialRank Editorial Team Updated: June 2026 Category: HVAC
Key Facts · June 2026
  • MRCOOL DIY is the only brand requiring no refrigerant certification — all others need EPA 608
  • Tools required: drill, hole saw (2.5–3 in), level, pipe bender (optional), electrical tools
  • Electrical requirement: dedicated 240V/20A or 240V/30A circuit depending on BTU size
  • Most common installation mistake: incorrect flare on the refrigerant line (conventional installs)
  • Permit required: in most US jurisdictions, including for MRCOOL DIY installations

Overview

Mini split installation differs from a window AC installation in one critical way: it requires a permanent electrical circuit and, for most brands, refrigerant handling. MRCOOL DIY eliminates the refrigerant step entirely with its pre-charged quick-connect lineset — the reason it is the dominant choice for homeowner installation. For conventional mini splits (all other brands), EPA 608 certification is required to purchase and handle refrigerant. This guide covers the MRCOOL DIY installation process, with notes on the additional steps required for conventional mini split installation.

How to Choose: Step by Step

1

Plan the installation: unit location and lineset path

The indoor head mounts on a wall, typically 7–8 feet from the floor. It must have clearance: 4 inches on sides, 8 inches above, no obstructions below the air intake. The lineset routes through a 2.5–3 inch hole in the wall to the outdoor unit. Plan the path to minimize lineset length (shorter = less pressure drop). The outdoor unit needs 12 inches clearance on all sides and 20 inches at the front for airflow.

2

Run the electrical circuit

Check the nameplate data plate on your unit for the MOCP (Maximum Overcurrent Protection) and MCA (Minimum Circuit Ampacity). Most 12K–24K units require a dedicated 240V/20A circuit; 36K units typically require 240V/30A. Run a new circuit from your panel using the correct wire gauge (12 AWG for 20A, 10 AWG for 30A). Install a local disconnect box within sight of the outdoor unit — required by NEC.

3

Mount the indoor unit and outdoor unit

Drill a pilot hole at the location of the wall penetration. Cut the 2.5–3 inch hole with a hole saw, angled 1/4 inch downward toward the outside for condensate drainage. Mount the indoor unit bracket level on solid framing — use a stud finder. Mount the outdoor unit on a concrete pad or wall bracket at least 4 inches off the ground to allow for ice and debris clearance.

4

Route the lineset and make connections

For MRCOOL DIY: thread the pre-charged lineset through the wall sleeve. Quick-connect the lineset fittings — hand-tighten only, do not use tools on the fittings. For conventional brands: flare the copper line ends using an HVAC flare tool, connect to the indoor unit flare nuts, route to the outdoor unit, and complete the refrigerant evacuation and charge process (requires EPA 608 and vacuum pump equipment).

5

Startup and commissioning

For MRCOOL DIY: after electrical connection, power the unit on and select cooling mode. The system will auto-purge and begin operating. For conventional brands: perform nitrogen pressure test, vacuum to 300 microns or below, then open the refrigerant service valves. Set thermostat to cooling and verify operation. Check for refrigerant leaks with a leak detector at all connection points.

Key Specifications Explained

MOCP
Maximum Overcurrent Protection — the maximum breaker or fuse size for the circuit. Never exceed this value.
MCA
Minimum Circuit Ampacity — the minimum wire and breaker ampacity for the circuit. Size to this or above.
Flare Fitting
The standard refrigerant connection type for conventional mini splits — a precision cone-shaped copper fitting. Must be made with an HVAC flare tool, not improvised.
Quick-Connect Fitting
MRCOOL DIY's proprietary sealed push-on refrigerant connector. Does not require any tools or EPA certification.
Micron Level
The vacuum level measured in microns of mercury (µHg). 300 microns or below is the standard for a leak-free refrigerant system before charging.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a permit to install a mini split?
Yes, in most US jurisdictions. A permit triggers an inspection, which verifies that the electrical connection was made correctly and the unit is operating properly. Unpermitted installations can void homeowner's insurance claims related to the installation and create liability when selling the property. MRCOOL DIY installations typically require an electrical permit at minimum.
How long does it take to install a mini split?
An experienced installer typically completes a single-zone installation in 4–6 hours. A first-time DIYer following MRCOOL's installation guide should plan for a full day. Multi-zone systems add 1–2 hours per additional zone. Most of the time is consumed by mounting, lineset routing, and electrical work — not the refrigerant connection.
Can I install a mini split without drilling through an exterior wall?
A wall penetration is required for the lineset and control wire to pass between the indoor and outdoor units. The penetration is 2.5–3 inches in diameter and can be made in any exterior wall. For locations where exterior drilling is impossible (masonry walls, historic structures), a window kit adapter is available for some mini split models.
What happens if I make a bad flare connection?
A poor flare on a conventional mini split connection allows refrigerant to leak. The system will gradually lose refrigerant, reducing performance over months or years. Most modern mini splits include a refrigerant leak indicator on the display. A bad flare can also allow moisture into the refrigerant circuit, causing compressor damage over time.
