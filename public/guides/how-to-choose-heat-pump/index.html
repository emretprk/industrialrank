How to Choose a Heat Pump in 2026

A heat pump replaces your air conditioner and furnace with one system. Getting the specifications right before you buy determines your comfort, your energy bills, and the system's lifespan.

By IndustrialRank Editorial Team Updated: June 2026 Category: HVAC
Key Facts · June 2026
  • Step 1: Determine your heating design temperature — this drives sizing and cold-climate unit selection
  • Step 2: Calculate heating and cooling loads with Manual J — never accept a square-footage guess
  • Step 3: Choose efficiency tier: single-stage (15 SEER2) vs variable-speed (18–22 SEER2)
  • Step 4: Verify cold-climate capability if your zone averages below 20°F for more than 2 weeks/year
  • Step 5: Check IRA Section 25C eligibility — up to $2,000 credit for ENERGY STAR certified systems

Overview

Heat pumps work by moving heat rather than generating it, achieving 2–4 BTU of heating output per BTU of electricity input — far more efficient than resistance heating. In cooling mode, they operate identically to a conventional air conditioner. The heat pump market has expanded rapidly since 2020, driven by the IRA tax credits, rising natural gas prices, and improved cold-climate technology. Today, buyers can choose from single-stage entry-level units at 15 SEER2 to variable-speed premium units reaching 22+ SEER2. For cold climates, dedicated cold-climate models now maintain full heating capacity at -13°F.

How to Choose: Step by Step

1

Calculate your heating and cooling load

The ACCA Manual J calculation is the standard for heat pump sizing. It accounts for your climate zone's design temperatures, your home's insulation levels, window area and orientation, air infiltration rate, and internal heat gains. Contractors who quote a size based only on square footage are guessing. Require a Manual J calculation — most HVAC contractors can produce one in 30–60 minutes.

2

Choose your efficiency tier

Single-stage heat pumps (15–16 SEER2) are the entry tier — they cost $1,500–$2,500 less upfront but consume 20–30% more energy annually than variable-speed units. Two-stage units offer a middle ground. Variable-speed (inverter-driven) units maintain temperature within 0.5°F, have lower noise levels, and achieve the highest dehumidification performance. For most buyers replacing a system they plan to keep for 15+ years, variable-speed pays back.

3

Assess cold-climate requirements

If your location sees more than two weeks of temperatures below 20°F per year, a standard heat pump is insufficient as your sole heat source. You need a cold-climate rated unit or a dual-fuel system pairing a heat pump with a gas furnace backup. The NEEP cold-climate heat pump listing is the authoritative reference for units meeting the performance specification.

4

Get three quotes from AHRI-certified contractors

Require AHRI-certified efficiency data for any system quoted. Ask for the Manual J calculation and the AHRI reference number. Compare installed costs, warranty terms (including labor warranty from the installer), and the equipment brand's repair parts availability in your area. Carrier and Lennox have denser dealer networks; Goodman and Daikin have the most widespread distribution.

5

Verify permits and inspection requirements

Every heat pump installation requires a building permit in most US jurisdictions. The permit triggers an inspection, which verifies correct refrigerant charge, electrical connection, and duct system condition. Unpermitted installations can create homeowner insurance issues and are a liability if you sell the property. Reputable contractors pull permits as a matter of course.

Key Specifications Explained

SEER2
Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio (2023 standard) — cooling efficiency. Federal minimum: 13–14 SEER2. Premium: 20–22 SEER2.
HSPF2
Heating Seasonal Performance Factor (2023 standard) — heating efficiency. Higher = lower heating bills. Cold-climate units reach 12+ HSPF2.
COP
Coefficient of Performance — instantaneous heating efficiency at a specific test condition. A COP of 3.0 means 3 BTU of heat output per BTU of electricity input.
Variable Speed
Also called inverter-driven. The compressor modulates from 25–100% capacity continuously, maintaining temperature within 0.5°F. More efficient and quieter than staged units.
Design Temperature
The 99% heating design temperature for your location — the temperature it will be colder than only 1% of winter hours. Used for sizing and cold-climate specification.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a heat pump cost in 2026?
Equipment cost ranges from $1,800–$4,500 for standard residential split systems. Installed cost (including labor, permit, and any required electrical work) is typically $4,000–$14,000. Variable-speed systems are at the higher end. After the IRA Section 25C tax credit ($2,000 maximum) and applicable utility rebates, net first cost can be significantly lower.
Is a heat pump better than a gas furnace?
For homes in climate zones 3–5 with electricity rates below $0.18/kWh, a heat pump has lower annual operating costs than a gas furnace. For zone 6–7 with high electricity rates, dual-fuel systems (heat pump + gas backup) optimize operating cost. Heat pumps also provide cooling, eliminating the need for a separate AC system.
Can a heat pump replace my existing furnace and AC?
Yes. A whole-home heat pump replaces both systems with one outdoor unit and one air handler. If your home has existing ductwork, a ducted heat pump can use it. If ductwork is undersized or absent, mini split heat pumps are installed without ductwork. The transition typically requires a new air handler and thermostat in addition to the outdoor unit.
What maintenance does a heat pump need?
Change or clean air filters monthly. Have the outdoor coil professionally cleaned every 2–3 years. Ensure the outdoor unit has at least 12 inches of clearance on all sides and is kept clear of leaves and debris. The refrigerant charge does not require periodic replenishment — a properly installed system should hold charge indefinitely. Annual professional tune-ups are recommended.
