Heat Pump Tax Credit 2026: Complete Guide

The IRA Section 25C credit covers 30% of qualifying heat pump costs up to $2,000 per year. Combined with state and utility rebates, total incentives for a new heat pump installation can exceed $5,000.

By IndustrialRank Editorial Team Updated: June 2026 Category: HVAC
Key Facts · June 2026
  • Federal tax credit: 30% of cost up to $2,000 under IRA Section 25C (available through 2032)
  • Eligible equipment: ENERGY STAR certified heat pumps meeting CEE Tier 1 specification
  • What counts toward the credit: equipment cost and installation labor both qualify
  • Income limit: none for Section 25C — available at all income levels
  • Cannot double-claim: if you receive a HEEHRA rebate for the same equipment, reduce your credit basis

Overview

The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 created and significantly expanded federal tax incentives for residential heat pumps. Section 25C provides a 30% nonrefundable tax credit up to $2,000 per year for qualifying heat pumps and heat pump water heaters. The credit is available through December 31, 2032 under current law, and resets annually — meaning a homeowner can claim up to $2,000 in one tax year, and claim again in subsequent years for additional qualifying equipment. Unlike the earlier non-business energy property credit, Section 25C has no lifetime cap.

How to Choose: Step by Step

1

Confirm the equipment qualifies

The heat pump must be ENERGY STAR certified and must meet the CEE (Consortium for Energy Efficiency) Tier 1 efficiency specification for your climate zone. For air-source heat pumps in northern states, the minimum is typically 15 SEER2 / 8.8 HSPF2. Verify on the ENERGY STAR certified products list before purchasing. The contractor should be able to provide the AHRI certificate number.

2

Understand what costs qualify

Both equipment cost and installation labor qualify for the credit. This includes the heat pump unit, the installation labor, any required electrical upgrades directly related to the heat pump installation, and refrigerant. Costs for ductwork, thermostats, or unrelated electrical work do not qualify unless they are directly required by the heat pump installation.

3

Calculate your credit amount

The credit is 30% of total qualifying costs, capped at $2,000 per year. For example: a $6,000 installed system (equipment + labor) qualifies for a $1,800 credit (30% of $6,000). A $10,000 installed system qualifies for a $2,000 credit (capped). The credit is nonrefundable — it can reduce your tax liability to zero but does not generate a refund if it exceeds your liability.

4

Check for additional rebates

The HEEHRA (High-Efficiency Electric Home Rebate Act) program, being implemented by states through 2024–2026, provides upfront rebates for qualifying households. Income-qualified households (below 150% of area median income) can receive additional rebates. The Section 25C credit and HEEHRA rebates can be combined, but the credit basis must be reduced by any rebate received.

5

File Form 5695 with your tax return

Claim the Section 25C credit on IRS Form 5695 (Residential Energy Credits). Enter the total qualifying costs in Part II. Keep all receipts, invoices, and the AHRI certificate number for the equipment. The credit carries forward to future years if it exceeds your current-year tax liability, under some circumstances — verify with a tax professional.

Key Specifications Explained

Section 25C
The IRS code section providing the 30% residential energy efficiency tax credit for heat pumps and other qualifying equipment through 2032.
ENERGY STAR Certified
Federal certification program. Equipment must appear on the ENERGY STAR certified products list to qualify for the credit.
CEE Tier 1
Consortium for Energy Efficiency efficiency tier — the minimum efficiency threshold for Section 25C qualification, varies by climate zone.
Nonrefundable Credit
A credit that reduces tax liability but cannot exceed it. If your credit is $2,000 but you owe $1,500, you save $1,500 — the remaining $500 is not paid out.
HEEHRA
High-Efficiency Electric Home Rebate Act — separate upfront rebate program for income-qualified households, reducing the initial purchase price.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the heat pump tax credit refundable?
No. The Section 25C credit is nonrefundable, meaning it can reduce your federal income tax liability to zero but cannot generate a refund. If your credit amount exceeds your tax liability, the excess is lost for that tax year. Plan your installation timing based on a tax year where you have sufficient liability to absorb the full credit.
Can I claim the heat pump tax credit and a state rebate?
Yes, in most cases. Federal and state incentives can be stacked. However, if you receive a HEEHRA rebate, the IRS requires you to reduce your Section 25C credit basis by the rebate amount. For example: $8,000 installed cost minus $2,000 HEEHRA rebate = $6,000 basis for the credit, yielding $1,800 instead of $2,000.
Does the heat pump need to be my primary heating system?
Yes. Section 25C requires the equipment to be installed in your primary residence (the home where you live most of the year). Vacation homes, rental properties, and new construction do not qualify for the Section 25C credit, though different incentives may apply.
What paperwork do I need to claim the credit?
Keep the purchase invoice showing itemized costs, the contractor's invoice showing labor costs, and the AHRI certificate number for the equipment. IRS Form 5695 requires the total qualifying cost — not a model number or efficiency rating. The IRS may request documentation in an audit; maintain records for at least three years after filing.
