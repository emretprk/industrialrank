Review

The Goodman 2-ton combo system at $2,053 sits squarely in the mid-range market for homeowners balancing budget and performance. This upflow configuration pairs a variable-speed air handler with a scroll compressor and dual-stage condenser for cooling, backed by an 80% AFUE gas furnace for heating. Energy Star certified with 16 SEER rating, it's positioned as a practical upgrade for contractors and DIY installers in regions where both seasons demand attention.

Specifications reveal solid part-load efficiency through its two-stage burner, two-stage condenser, and variable-speed blower reaching 2,000 CFM. The R410A refrigerant system with 24,000 cooling BTU and 24,000 heating BTU matches the 2-ton cooling capacity, though tonnage labeling conflicts in specs deserve clarification. At 72 dB and 208/230V single-phase operation, it handles typical residential electrical panels without special upgrades. The metal flue requirement and sweat valve connections demand proper installation.

Pros & Cons

Pros

  • 16 SEER rating delivers solid cooling efficiency with Energy Star certification, reducing summer energy bills by 15-20% versus 13 SEER units
  • Two-stage burner and two-stage condenser provide quieter operation at 72 dB and better part-load efficiency during shoulder seasons
  • Variable-speed blower motor optimizes airflow at 2,000 CFM maximum and reduces fan noise, improving comfort without constant cycling
  • Dual-fuel capability handles both natural gas and propane, offering flexibility for rural and urban installations
  • 10-year warranty with online registration provides solid coverage for compressor, parts, and labor on the sealed system

Cons

  • 80% AFUE furnace efficiency is outdated by modern standards, where 95%+ AFUE models are increasingly common and cost-effective
  • Specs show conflicting tonnage information, listing both 2-ton and 3-ton, which creates confusion about actual capacity and matching requirements
  • 72 dB noise level is moderate-to-loud for residential settings, especially during high-stage operation on hot days

Who Should Buy

Best For

Homeowners in transition zones needing both cooling and heating who want to control upfront costs without sacrificing efficiency. Ideal for those replacing 10+ year old split systems where the furnace still functions adequately but the air conditioner needs replacement.

Consider Alternatives If

Skip this if you prioritize heating efficiency, need a high-SEER system for hot climates with minimal heating needs, or require quieter operation below 65 dB.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this really a 2-ton or 3-ton system? +
Specs list conflicting information, but 24,000 cooling and heating BTU equals 2-ton capacity (12,000 BTU per ton). The 3-ton label appears erroneous. Confirm tonnage with the seller before purchase to ensure proper ductwork matching and load calculations.
Can I install this myself if I'm experienced with HVAC? +
Upflow configuration simplifies installation compared to downflow units. However, refrigerant handling requires EPA Section 608 certification, and flue venting demands precision. Professional installation ensures warranty validity and avoids costly mistakes.
How does 16 SEER compare to newer standards? +
16 SEER is solid mid-tier efficiency, roughly 30% better than baseline 13 SEER units. However, newer 18-22 SEER models now exist. For moderate climates, 16 SEER justifies the cost versus premium options, saving $30-50 monthly on cooling versus older systems.
Why is 80% AFUE included if high-efficiency furnaces exist? +
Budget constraint. This combo targets budget-first buyers where furnace replacement isn't urgent. Many contractors pair this with existing furnaces anyway. If heating dominates your utility bills, seek 95%+ AFUE units despite higher upfront cost.
What electrical service do I need? +
Unit requires 208/230V single-phase 60Hz with minimum 15.8-amp breaker and maximum 25-amp breaker. Most residential panels accommodate this without upgrade, making electrical integration straightforward.
