Review

The Goodman GPHM34241 is a workhorse 3.5 ton packaged heat pump that occupies the comfortable middle ground in the HVAC market. At $4,671, it undercuts premium brands while delivering credible efficiency improvements over systems installed before 2018. This unit targets the practical contractor and homeowner who value straightforward installation and dependable performance without paying for cutting-edge variable-speed technology or smart integrations.

Performance specs are respectable across the board. The unit produces 41,000 BTU of cooling and 40,600 BTU of heating, handling standard residential loads without oversizing. The 13.4 SEER2 rating reflects modern efficiency standards and should lower cooling costs by 15 to 20 percent compared to 10.5 SEER equipment from 2010. An HSPF of 8 means heating performance adequate for climates where winter lows stay above 20 degrees Fahrenheit most days. The scroll compressor design, paired with the ECM blower motor capable of 1,300 CFM maximum, balances reliability with seasonal variability.

Pros & Cons

Pros

  • 13.4 SEER2 efficiency rating exceeds most models in the $4,500 to $5,000 range by 0.5 to 1.0 points
  • Multi-position design handles downflow and horizontal installations, covering roughly 85% of residential applications

Installation

This multi-position unit suits both downflow and horizontal installation configurations, simplifying retrofit work. Electrical requirements demand a 29.6 to 45 amp breaker on 208 to 230 volt single-phase power, standard in most residential panels. Weight at 410 pounds requires two people and basic lifting equipment. Ductwork compatibility remains critical, as packaged units don't tolerate significant undersizing.

Warranty & Reliability

Goodman backs this unit with a 10-year parts warranty once you complete online registration, a meaningful commitment for a mid-tier product. Coverage protects against compressor failure and component defects but excludes labor and maintenance neglect. Registration takes minutes and should be completed before installation to activate full benefits.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does the 13.4 SEER2 rating compare to similar tonnage units? +
This rating places the unit slightly above average for packaged 3.5 ton systems. Most competitors in the $4,000 to $5,500 range score between 12.5 and 14.0 SEER2. Single-stage models like this one typically lag two-stage alternatives by 0.5 to 1.5 points, though price differences often exceed that efficiency gap.
Can I install this myself, or do I need a licensed technician? +
Refrigerant charging requires EPA Section 608 certification by law. However, non-refrigerant tasks like ductwork connections and electrical work can be DIY-friendly if you're comfortable with construction. Most homeowners hire contractors for the full job, averaging 4 to 6 hours of labor.
Will this system handle my heating needs without backup heat? +
At 8 HSPF, it works without auxiliary heat in climates where winter minimums rarely drop below 25 degrees Fahrenheit. For colder regions, adding 5 to 15 kilowatt backup electric heat is practical, though it increases monthly costs during deep freezes.
How does this compare to Bryant or Carrier units at the same price? +
Goodman typically undercuts premium brands by $800 to $1,500 on packaged units while delivering comparable SEER2 and HSPF ratings. The trade-off is single-stage compression and less brand cachet, not performance. Choose Goodman if budget drives the decision, premium brands if resale value matters significantly.
What happens if something fails after the 10-year warranty expires? +
Coverage ends completely after 10 years. Repair costs vary but a scroll compressor replacement typically runs $1,500 to $2,500. Extended warranties from dealers can sometimes stretch protection, but they're often poor value past year 5.
