Review

The Mitsubishi MSZ-GS24NA represents a solid mid-range option in the mini-split market, particularly for contractors managing multi-zone installations. This quad-zone configuration leverages Mitsubishi's reputation for reliable heating performance, combining efficient cooling with dependable winter operation. The included wireless controller and straightforward wall-mounted design appeal to both installers seeking quick commissioning and homeowners wanting modern comfort controls.

Performance metrics tell a compelling story here. The 21.5 SEER2 rating sits well above baseline efficiency standards, while the 27,600 BTU heating capacity demonstrates genuine cold-climate capability. The unit delivers 22,400 BTU cooling and operates across a 208/230v single-phase circuit. With an EER of 13.4 and variable CFM output ranging from 336 to 701 cubic feet per minute, this equipment handles partial-load scenarios efficiently, which matters for zoned systems where not all areas demand simultaneous operation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does the 6+6+12+24 zone configuration actually mean? +
This denotes four separate indoor wall units with BTU capacities of 6,000, 6,000, 12,000, and 24,000 respectively. Each zone operates independently from a single outdoor unit, allowing individual temperature control across different rooms or floors. Total system capacity reaches 48,000 BTU combined output.
Can I install this in an older home with 100-amp service? +
Yes, but verify available breaker space first. The 40-45 amp breaker requirement is manageable in most 100-amp panels, though a licensed electrician should confirm your panel's capacity. Some older homes may need service upgrades, which becomes a separate cost consideration.
How does 21.5 SEER2 compare to my current system? +
SEER2 testing uses more realistic conditions than older SEER ratings. A 21.5 SEER2 unit typically delivers 25-30 percent better efficiency than 16 SEER equipment. For a 2-ton system running 500 annual cooling hours, expect measurable energy bill reductions, potentially $200-400 annually depending on your local electricity rates.
Is this better than installing four separate single-zone systems? +
Not necessarily cheaper upfront, but superior for energy efficiency and simplicity. One outdoor unit costs less than four, uses less refrigerant, and requires simpler electrical infrastructure. You sacrifice redundancy though, meaning a single compressor failure affects all zones until repair.
Does the wireless controller work through walls? +
Yes, standard wireless remotes operate through walls and doors at typical residential distances. However, signal strength depends on interference from walls, metal studs, and other electronics. Most installations experience reliable 30-50 foot range without issues.
