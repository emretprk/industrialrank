Review

The Goodman 5-ton gas furnace and heat pump system represents a mainstream choice for homeowners balancing efficiency gains against replacement costs. This upflow configuration handles both heating and cooling, making it suitable for all-in-one system upgrades. The Energy Star certification signals compliance with current efficiency benchmarks, though the hardware itself sits in the solid mid-range rather than premium tier. Goodman has maintained brand presence through contractor relationships and parts availability, despite recent quality control debates in the industry.

Cooling performance delivers 57,000 BTU at 16.7 SEER1 with a scroll compressor and two-stage condenser, enabling the unit to modulate capacity during mild weather rather than cycling on-off constantly. The heating side produces 57,000 BTU output at 80% AFUE with two-stage burners, meaning roughly 80 cents of every energy dollar converts to usable heat, with 20 cents lost through flue gases. The variable-speed blower motor and maximum 2,000 CFM airflow help distribute conditioned air more evenly than fixed-speed alternatives. Electrical demands run 38.8 to 60 amps at 208/230V single-phase, typical for residential installations.

Installation

This upflow unit measures 30 inches tall, 21 inches wide and deep, fitting most standard closets and utility spaces. Installation requires metal exhaust flue, proper R410A or R22 refrigerant line sizing (3/8 inch liquid, 1 1/8 inch suction with 1/2 inch insulation), and 208/230V electrical service with appropriate breaker protection. Professional HVAC installation is strongly recommended for refrigerant charging, ductwork connections, and gas line safety verification.

Warranty & Reliability

Goodman provides 10 years of parts coverage upon online registration, covering compressor, coils, and most components. This warranty is standard for the brand but shorter than some competitors offering 12-15 years. Labor and refrigerant are typically not covered. Registration immediately after purchase is essential to activate protection and should be documented.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between SEER1 16.7 and SEER2 ratings I see on newer units? +
SEER1 and SEER2 use different testing methods; SEER2 reflects real-world conditions more accurately and typically runs 15-20% lower numerically. This unit's 16.7 SEER1 roughly equals approximately 13.3-14 SEER2, placing it mid-market rather than premium tier.
Can this system work with my existing ductwork? +
The 2,000 CFM maximum airflow rate is standard for 5-ton systems. Existing ductwork should be evaluated by a technician to confirm it handles this volume without excessive static pressure, which reduces efficiency and comfort.
Is 80% AFUE heating good enough for my region? +
80% AFUE is code-compliant in most climates but misses efficiency bonuses available in zones 6-8 with 90%+ units. In milder climates (zones 4-5), the lower upfront cost often outweighs modest heating season savings from higher AFUE.
How does this Goodman compare to Lennox or Carrier equivalents at the same tonnage? +
This unit competes on price but lags slightly on cooling efficiency and noise ratings. Lennox and Carrier 5-ton systems often hit 17-18 SEER1 and operate 2-3 dB quieter, though at higher cost and with better resale value perception.
What happens after the 10-year warranty expires? +
Parts are no longer covered by manufacturer warranty. Repair costs become the homeowner's responsibility, though Goodman parts remain widely available and relatively affordable through HVAC wholesalers.
