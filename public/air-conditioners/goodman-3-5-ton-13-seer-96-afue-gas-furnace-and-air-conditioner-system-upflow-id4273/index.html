// Bottom Line
This Goodman gas furnace and AC combo works best for homeowners replacing aging systems who want reliable comfort without premium pricing. The 96% AFUE and 13 SEER ratings deliver measurable energy savings, making it ideal for mid-range budget retrofits in upflow configurations.

Review

The Goodman 3.5 ton upflow system pairs a 96% AFUE gas furnace with a 13 SEER air conditioner, making it a straightforward replacement for contractors upgrading homes from older split-system equipment. This unit competes directly with mid-range Rheem and Armstrong offerings, delivering reliable performance without premium-brand pricing. The A-coil configuration and upflow orientation fit standard residential installations where ductwork already exists.

Heating output hits a solid 100,000 BTU via two-stage burners, allowing the furnace to ramp down during mild weather and reduce energy waste. The multi-speed ECM blower can push up to 1,600 CFM, adapting airflow to actual heating and cooling demand. However, the 42,000 cooling BTU output reveals a spec inconsistency with the 3.5 ton tonnage claim, suggesting this unit works better for smaller homes or as a supplemental cooler in mixed-climate regions.

Pros & Cons

Pros

  • 96% AFUE furnace efficiency means lower heating bills, recovering equipment cost in 3 to 5 years for most homeowners
  • 13 SEER air conditioning matches modern efficiency standards and qualifies for Energy Star certification
  • Two-stage burners and multi-speed ECM blower motor optimize comfort and reduce short-cycling
  • Lifetime heat exchanger warranty provides genuine peace of mind on the most expensive failure point
  • PVC flue compatibility simplifies installation and reduces venting material costs versus metal ductwork

Cons

  • Single-stage compressor limits seasonal efficiency gains compared to variable-capacity competitors
  • Reciprocating compressor design generates 75 dBA noise, which some owners find noticeable during operation
  • 42,000 cooling BTU falls short for larger homes or southern climates requiring true 3.5 ton output

Who Should Buy

Best For

Contractors and homeowners in moderate climates seeking a balance between efficiency and upfront cost. This system works especially well for homes with existing ductwork and upflow furnace installations where budget constraints favor Goodman over high-end Lennox or Carrier equipment.

Consider Alternatives If

Buyers in hot climates needing maximum AC capacity should look elsewhere, since the 42,000 cooling BTU undershoots the stated 3.5 ton rating.

Installation

Upflow and horizontal mounting options suit basement and attic placements common in North American homes. The unit requires 115V single-phase 60Hz power with a 23.9 to 40 amp breaker, standard for residential circuits. Sweat-type fittings on the 3/8 inch liquid line and 7/8 inch suction line demand competent HVAC technician work; DIY installation is not recommended.

Warranty & Reliability

Goodman's warranty covers the heat exchanger for life, unit replacement for 10 years, and parts for 10 years with online registration. This three-tier structure protects the most costly failure point while incentivizing preventive maintenance. Real-world claims experience shows solid coverage, though labor costs remain the buyer's responsibility after year one.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does this unit really deliver 3.5 tons of cooling? +
The 42,000 cooling BTU output equals roughly 3.5 tons by formula, but this underperforms typical residential expectations for that tonnage rating. In practice, expect cooling capacity closer to 3 tons in humid climates. Check Manual J calculations for your home before purchasing.
What electrical upgrades do I need? +
The system runs on standard 115V household power with a minimum 23.9 amp breaker and maximum 40 amp capacity. Most homes have existing circuits suitable for this draw. Your electrician should verify breaker availability and wire gauge during the site survey.
How much will this save on heating bills versus my old furnace? +
At 96% AFUE, this furnace converts 96 cents of every dollar spent on gas into heat. An old 78% AFUE unit wastes roughly 22% more fuel. Savings typically range from 15 to 20 percent annually, translating to 200 to 400 dollars per year in moderate climates.
How does this compare to Lennox or Carrier systems at similar prices? +
Goodman trades brand prestige for lower cost without sacrificing core efficiency ratings. A Lennox equivalent costs 30 to 40 percent more upfront while delivering similar SEER and AFUE performance. Goodman suits budget-conscious buyers comfortable with solid, no-frills reliability.
Is the lifetime heat exchanger warranty actually valuable? +
Yes, it addresses the single most expensive failure point in a furnace. Heat exchanger replacement typically costs 1,000 to 2,000 dollars, making this coverage genuinely worthwhile over 15 to 20 years of ownership.
