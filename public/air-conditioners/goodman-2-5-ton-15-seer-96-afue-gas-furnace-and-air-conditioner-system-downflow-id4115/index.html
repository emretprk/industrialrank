Review

The 2.5 ton Goodman downflow system from Online Supply sits squarely in the budget segment at $643, targeting homeowners and contractors who need functional climate control without premium pricing. This packaged unit combines a gas furnace with an air conditioner in a single configuration, eliminating the need for separate component selection. Energy Star certification signals baseline efficiency compliance, while the downflow configuration suits homes where ductwork runs below the unit or in attic spaces.

Performance specs reveal a capable mid-range system: the furnace delivers 40,000 BTU heating with 96% AFUE efficiency using dual-stage burners, meaning it runs lower flame during mild weather to conserve fuel. Cooling capacity reaches 29,000 BTU with a SEER1 rating of 16 and single-stage scroll compressor technology. The variable-speed ECM blower motor operates at 1200 CFM maximum with adaptive speed control that reduces energy waste during partial load conditions, a feature typically reserved for higher-priced equipment.

Installation

Installation requires 115V single-phase 60Hz power with 17.1 to 25 amp breaker sizing, standard for residential circuits. The unit accepts both R410A and R22 refrigerants, though new installations should specify R410A. Downflow configuration works with plastic or PVC exhaust flues, common in modern homes. Proper ductwork must support the 1200 CFM airflow, so undersized existing ducts will need replacement to achieve rated performance.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does the product title say 2.5 ton but specs list 3 ton tonnage? +
This appears to be a listing error from Online Supply. The actual tonnage is 3 ton based on 29,000 BTU cooling capacity. Verify before purchasing if exact tonnage matters for your home's load calculation. Request clarification from the seller to confirm which rating applies to your installation.
Can I install this system if my current ductwork is smaller than 1200 CFM capacity? +
Technically yes, but you'll lose efficiency and comfort. The 1200 CFM maximum output requires properly sized ducts to avoid excess static pressure, which forces the blower to work harder and consumes more electricity. Undersized ducts typically cost $1500-3000 to upgrade but recover costs through improved efficiency.
How does 96% AFUE compare to newer furnaces, and will it reduce my heating bills? +
96% AFUE is excellent, capturing 96 cents of every dollar spent on fuel as heat versus older 80% units that waste 20%. If replacing a 10-year-old 80% furnace, expect 15-20% heating cost reduction annually, translating to $200-400 savings depending on climate and fuel prices.
Is this system better than splitting the purchase into separate condenser and furnace units? +
Packaged systems simplify installation and reduce labor costs, but separate units often allow better efficiency matching and future upgrades. At this price point, the packaged option makes sense. Upgrade flexibility matters more if you plan to keep the system 15-20 years versus 10.
Does the 10-year warranty cover refrigerant leaks or electrical failures? +
Standard coverage includes manufacturing defects in sealed components and electrical parts. Refrigerant leaks from copper line corrosion or punctures often exclude coverage. Review the AHRI certificate number 7368837 and full warranty document before purchase to confirm specific exclusions.
