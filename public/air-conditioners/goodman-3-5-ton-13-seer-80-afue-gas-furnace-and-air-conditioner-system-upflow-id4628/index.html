Review

Goodman's 3.5 ton combination system represents the practical middle ground in residential HVAC. This upflow unit pairs a gas furnace with an air conditioning condenser, offering homeowners and contractors a straightforward replacement solution. While not cutting-edge, the system's proven Goodman reliability and accessible pricing make it popular for budget-conscious installations where efficiency gains matter less than simplicity and affordability.

Performance metrics show competent, if unspectacular, capability across both heating and cooling. The furnace delivers 100,000 BTU output with two-stage combustion and 80% AFUE efficiency, meaning 20% of fuel energy escapes through the flue. The matched condenser provides 42,000 BTU cooling capacity with a 13 SEER rating, translating to reasonable summer efficiency. The variable-speed blower motor running up to 2,000 CFM helps offset single-stage compressor limitations, though it never reaches premium multi-stage modulation.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does 13 SEER compare to modern air conditioners? +
13 SEER represents entry-level modern cooling efficiency. New systems typically range 14-18 SEER. Each SEER point difference costs roughly 3-5% more annually in electricity. For 2,000-hour cooling seasons, that's $150-300 yearly variance in moderate climates. Acceptable for budget replacements, suboptimal for long-term ownership.
Can this system use both R410A and R22 refrigerant? +
The specs note compatibility with R410A or R22, but confirm actual refrigerant type at purchase. R22 units are older discontinued models increasingly difficult to service. Most modern Goodman systems use R410A exclusively. Installation technicians should verify refrigerant type before commissioning to ensure proper oil and component compatibility.
Will the variable-speed blower reduce electricity bills significantly? +
Variable-speed blowers modulate output to match heating and cooling demand, improving part-load efficiency by 10-15% versus single-speed motors. However, single-stage compressor limits overall system modulation. Real savings depend on climate and usage patterns. Moderate climates see 8-12% furnace fan energy reduction, but cooling savings remain modest due to compressor constraints.
How does 80% AFUE impact long-term heating costs? +
80% AFUE means 20 cents of every heating dollar escapes unburned through the flue. Modern 95% AFUE systems waste only 5 cents. Over 20 years in cold climates, efficiency differences cost $3,000-5,000 extra in natural gas. Better for mild climates or budget-conscious owners accepting higher operating expenses.
Is the 10-year warranty adequate protection? +
Ten years matches baseline industry standards. Most component failures occur in years 2-7, so coverage is reasonable. However, compressors occasionally fail beyond year 10 when costs escalate. Extended warranties or dealer maintenance plans provide added peace of mind beyond the base registration requirement.
