Review

Goodman's modulating gas furnace and air conditioner combination targets efficiency-conscious homeowners seeking better comfort control than older equipment provides. This downflow configuration works in basements, crawlspaces, or utility closets where horizontal airflow suits the home's layout. The 97% AFUE rating and Energy Star certification signal solid engineering, while the two-stage condenser approach differentiates it from basic single-stage competitors. Online Supply distributes this unit under SKU LS38783850 with AHRI verification number 7424269.

The furnace delivers 60,000 BTU of heat using modulating burner technology, which gradually adjusts flame level rather than simply cycling on-off. The air conditioner produces 35,000 BTU cooling at 18 SEER1 efficiency through a two-stage scroll compressor and variable-speed ECM blower rated for 1,200 CFM maximum. This combination achieves better part-load performance than fixed-capacity systems, particularly in shoulder seasons when heating or cooling demand fluctuates hourly. Electrical requirements are moderate at 208-230V single-phase, requiring a 21.3 to 35 amp breaker.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does the spec sheet show conflicting tonnage ratings? +
The product header lists 3 ton, but cooling specifications show 35,000 BTU output with 5 ton designation. This likely reflects a documentation error from the manufacturer or distributor. Confirm actual cooling capacity with your installer and match it to your home's square footage to avoid undersizing.
Can this furnace vent through plastic PVC pipe like newer condensing units? +
Yes, the 97% AFUE rating indicates a condensing furnace, which produces cooler exhaust suitable for PVC venting. This simplifies installation versus high-temperature metal flues, reduces venting material costs, and often allows shorter vent runs to exterior walls.
How does the modulating burner improve efficiency compared to standard two-stage furnaces? +
Modulating burners adjust output continuously between minimum and maximum firing rates, matching heating demand rather than cycling full-on or full-off. This prevents temperature swings, reduces fuel consumption, and extends equipment life by minimizing thermal stress on components.
Is 60,000 BTU enough to heat a 3,000-square-foot home in a cold climate? +
No. As a rule-of-thumb, homes need 40 to 60 BTU per square foot depending on climate and insulation. 60,000 BTU suits homes up to roughly 1,500-1,800 square feet in moderate climates. Colder regions or larger homes require 80,000-100,000 BTU or higher.
What warranty protection applies if I don't register the unit online? +
Unregistered units typically receive shorter warranty coverage, sometimes just 5 years instead of 10. Register immediately after installation to activate full coverage. Keep documentation handy in case you need to claim repairs within the warranty period.
