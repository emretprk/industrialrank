Review

The ACiQ 3.5 ton upflow furnace with 97% AFUE represents a solid mid-market choice for contractors and homeowners seeking genuine efficiency improvements without boutique pricing. Built on proven Goodman platform reliability, this unit combines a modulating furnace with a single-stage scroll compressor condenser, making it versatile for most residential applications. The variable speed ECM blower and plastic flue compatibility signal thoughtful engineering for real-world install constraints.

Heating output reaches 120,000 BTU with modulating burner stages that match capacity to actual demand, reducing wasted fuel during mild weather. Cooling delivers 42,000 BTU at 14 SEER using R410A refrigerant and a scroll compressor. The variable speed blower peaks at 2000 CFM while running quieter than fixed-speed motors, with dual fuel capability (natural gas and propane) adding flexibility. Electrical requirements sit at 208-230V single phase with 22-35 amp breaker range, standard for most residential panels.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much can I expect to save on heating costs with 97% AFUE versus my old 80% furnace? +
A 17-point AFUE jump reduces heating fuel consumption by approximately 21% annually. On average heating costs of $1,200 yearly, expect savings around $250-300 in cold climates, less in mild zones. Real savings depend on usage patterns, fuel prices, and how well you maintain the unit. Most homeowners recover the efficiency upgrade cost in 3-5 years through energy savings.
Can this furnace and condenser work in my home with PVC ductwork instead of metal? +
Yes, this model specifically rates for plastic PVC flue venting, which simplifies installations where metal venting conflicts with layout constraints. However, PVC applies to flue exhaust only, not ductwork. Cold air returns and heated supply ducts still use standard flexible or rigid ductwork. Confirm local code approval before installation, as some jurisdictions restrict PVC flue in certain applications.
What's the difference between 14 SEER and 15-16 SEER for cooling performance? +
Each SEER point represents roughly 3.5% cooling efficiency gain. This 14 SEER unit runs 6-9% less efficient than newer 15-16 SEER models over an entire cooling season. In hot climates, that difference translates to $200-400 extra yearly cooling costs. For moderate climates where cooling matters less, the performance gap becomes negligible relative to furnace heating benefits.
How does single-stage cooling compare to two-stage in this price range? +
Single-stage compressors run full capacity or off with no middle ground, cycling more frequently and using more energy during partial-load conditions. Two-stage units run low capacity most of the time, reducing wear and energy use. This unit's advantage is simplicity and lower repair costs. Two-stage adds $500-800 to price but delivers 1-2 SEER improvement and quieter operation.
Is the 10-year warranty enough coverage for a furnace? +
10-year parts warranty covers most component failures during the critical period when defects surface. Modern furnaces typically run 15-20 years, so you lose coverage in years 11 and beyond. This aligns with budget-market standards. Consider extended warranties or service plans if you want protection beyond 10 years, especially for compressor replacement.
