Review

The ACiQ 2 Ton combo system represents Goodman's practical approach to mid-market HVAC replacement. At $2,666, it positions itself between entry-level commodity equipment and premium variable-capacity systems. This upflow A-coil configuration delivers 80,000 BTU heating and 24,000 BTU cooling (technically 2.5 tons), making it suitable for 1,500-2,000 square foot homes across most U.S. climate zones. The Energy Star certification and rebate availability address the cost factor that drives many replacement decisions.

Performance metrics show 96% AFUE on the heating side with two-stage burner modulation, meaning it runs efficiently during cold snaps without wasting fuel during mild winter days. The 13 SEER1 cooling efficiency is respectable but not exceptional, reflecting the single-stage compressor design. Variable-speed ECM blower motor operates at 1,200 CFM maximum and adjusts automatically, improving comfort and humidity management compared to fixed-speed alternatives. The 75 dB noise level is audible from inside the home during peak operation.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does 13 SEER1 compare to SEER2 ratings I'm seeing elsewhere? +
SEER1 and SEER2 use different test conditions; SEER2 typically reads 15-20% lower than SEER1. This system's 13 SEER1 roughly equals 10-11 SEER2, placing it in the mid-efficiency category. Check manufacturer documentation for direct SEER2 ratings if comparing across brands using new standards.
Can this unit replace my existing 3-ton system, or will undersizing cause problems? +
The 2-ton (technically 2.5-ton) capacity requires proper load calculation using Manual J standards. Undersizing causes inadequate cooling and heating, while oversizing causes short-cycling and poor humidity control. Hire a contractor to calculate your home's actual load before purchasing.
Why does this have two tonnages listed, 2 Ton and 2.5 Ton? +
The cooling capacity is 24,000 BTU, which equals 2 tons exactly. Industry convention sometimes rounds nominal ratings; this unit genuinely delivers 2-ton cooling. The 2.5-ton reference may reflect heating capacity or manufacturer classification, so confirm actual cooling BTU.
Is the single-stage compressor a major limitation compared to variable-capacity systems? +
Single-stage compressors cycle on and off rather than adjusting capacity smoothly, reducing efficiency during light-load conditions. Variable systems shine in moderate weather and partial-load scenarios. Single-stage is adequate for consistent climate control but less efficient seasonally.
What does the $2,666 price exclude that I should budget for? +
This covers only the condenser unit and furnace/coil combination. Budget separately for refrigerant lines, installation labor, electrical panel modifications if needed, ductwork adjustments, and disposal of old equipment. Total installed cost typically reaches $5,500-$7,500 depending on location and complexity.
