Review

The Goodman 2.5-ton combination system represents a practical entry point for homeowners replacing older HVAC equipment on a tight budget. At $736, this upflow-configured unit bundles a gas furnace and air conditioner from a manufacturer with decades of contractor trust. The compact 30 by 21 by 21 inch footprint fits standard closets and utility spaces, while the dual-stage furnace burner and multi-speed blower address real comfort needs during partial-load operation.

Performance specifications tell a balanced efficiency story. The AC unit delivers 30,000 BTU cooling capacity with 14 SEER efficiency, a respectable baseline that avoids premium pricing while meeting current code minimums. The furnace generates 100,000 BTU output at 80% AFUE, consuming slightly more gas than high-efficiency units but requiring no condensing exhaust line or special venting. The rotary compressor operates on standard R410A refrigerant, and the 2000 CFM maximum airflow suits 2.5-ton applications without undersizing risk.

Installation

Installation requires standard electrical service at 120V, 1-phase, 60 Hz with 17.1 to 25-amp breaker capacity, matching most residential panels without upgrade. The system uses conventional 3/8-inch liquid lines and 3/4-inch suction lines with pre-insulated 50-foot line set included. Upflow configuration suits basement or closet placement, though the metal exhaust flue requirement means routing ductwork through existing chimneys or new wall penetrations.

Warranty & Reliability

The 10-year parts warranty activates upon online registration and covers compressor, furnace heat exchanger, and blower motor failures. This standard coverage protects against defects but excludes normal wear, refrigerant loss, and labor costs. Online registration is straightforward and highly recommended to activate the full term.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is this system cheaper than others at the same tonnage? +
The lower price reflects older efficiency technology, single-stage cooling that cycles on/off rather than modulating, and an 80% AFUE furnace instead of 90%+ condensing models. You save upfront capital but spend more on utility bills over 10 years, typical in budget HVAC products.
Can this system use my existing ductwork from my old furnace? +
Usually yes, if your old system was also 2.5-ton or larger and used similar trunk duct sizing. The 2000 CFM airflow is standard for this tonnage. Have a contractor verify duct balance since inadequate return air causes short-cycling and comfort problems.
Will this AC unit keep my house cool in 95-degree heat? +
The 30,000 BTU output handles moderate heat in well-insulated homes. In high-heat climates or poorly sealed homes, cooling demand may exceed capacity, causing runtime extension and reduced comfort. Higher SEER units with variable capacity perform better in extremes.
How does 14 SEER compare to newer systems I'm seeing advertised? +
14 SEER is baseline modern efficiency, while newer systems reach 18-20 SEER with variable-speed compressors. You'll recoup the $1500+ premium on 18+ SEER units within 5-7 years through lower electric bills in hot climates, but in mild regions, 14 SEER remains cost-effective.
Does the 10-year warranty cover refrigerant leaks? +
The warranty covers compressor failure from manufacturing defects, but refrigerant loss from leaks is excluded unless caused by factory defect. Service calls and evacuation labor are homeowner costs, typically $300-600 per incident.
