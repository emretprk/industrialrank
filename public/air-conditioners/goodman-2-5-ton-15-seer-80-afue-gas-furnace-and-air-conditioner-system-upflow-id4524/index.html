Review

Goodman's 2.5 ton upflow gas furnace and air conditioner system occupies a pragmatic middle ground in the residential HVAC market. At $2,552, it positions itself between budget-conscious contractors and homeowners unwilling to spend $4,000+ on premium equipment. The Energy Star certification and two-stage burner design signal genuine engineering rather than basic components wrapped in marketing. This is the workhorse equipment you'll find on tens of thousands of residential jobs annually, prized for standardization and serviceable parts availability.

The heating side delivers 60,000 BTU through dual-stage burners at 80% AFUE, meaning approximately 48,000 BTU reaches the home while 12,000 BTU exits through a metal flue. The cooling condenser produces 29,000 BTU with 16 SEER1 efficiency rating (outpacing the advertised 15 SEER), paired with a scroll compressor running at single-stage. The variable-speed blower motor reaches 1,600 CFM maximum, enabling quieter ramp-up versus fixed-speed alternatives. At 71.5 dBA, noise levels remain acceptable for residential settings though not whisper-quiet.

Pros & Cons

Pros

  • Variable speed blower motor maxes out at 1600 CFM, enabling quieter operation and better humidity control than single-stage competitors
  • 80% AFUE furnace with two-stage burners provides genuine heating efficiency gains in cold climates without approaching premium pricing
  • 16 SEER1 air conditioner significantly exceeds the 15 SEER stated in title, indicating actual condenser performance outpaces marketing claims
  • Two-stage burners reduce cycling losses and deliver more consistent comfort during partial-load heating conditions
  • 10-year warranty with online registration covers parts comprehensively for a system at this price point

Cons

  • 80% AFUE is respectable but trails 95%+ AFUE high-efficiency units, meaning higher heating bills over the system's lifespan in severe climates

Installation

Installation requires standard three-ton clearances and upflow ducting configuration. The system accepts both R410A and R22 refrigerants, offering flexibility with existing line sets. Metal exhaust flue is mandatory, ruling out flexible venting. Electrical demand peaks at 25 amps maximum, so verify your breaker panel has available capacity. A-coil design means evaporator placement inside the furnace, simplifying indoor unit coordination.

Warranty & Reliability

Goodman provides 10-year comprehensive parts coverage upon online registration, though labor and refrigerant typically fall outside scope. This matches industry standards for mid-tier systems. Extended warranties are available separately but rarely cost-justified given the 15-20 year typical equipment lifespan. Evaporator coil coverage extends beyond standard terms, protecting your costliest indoor component.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does this system qualify for government rebates or tax credits? +
Possibly, depending on your location and current incentive programs. Energy Star certification helps, though many newer models hit 17+ SEER to capture higher rebates. Check local utility companies and DSIRE.org for your state. The 80% AFUE may disqualify you from some heating-focused incentives requiring 95%+ efficiency.
Can I install this myself, or do I need a licensed technician? +
Most jurisdictions legally require EPA-certified technicians for refrigerant handling and system commissioning. DIY installation voids warranty and creates liability. Installation typically costs $1,200-$2,000 professionally, so factor that into your total cost analysis before comparing to higher-efficiency alternatives.
How does 80% AFUE compare to my old furnace and newer models? +
Furnaces from 2005 operate around 78-80% AFUE, so you'll gain minimal heating efficiency upgrading to this unit. Modern 95% AFUE models save 15-20% on heating bills but cost $2,000-$3,000 more upfront. For 15-year payback math, calculate your local heating degree days and gas rates.
Is single-stage cooling adequate, or should I demand a two-stage unit? +
Single-stage works fine in mild climates with moderate cooling loads. In hot regions, two-stage compressors reduce cycling losses and improve dehumidification. At this price point, upgrading to modulating capacity typically costs $800-$1,200 extra, narrowing the value advantage of this budget unit.
What if the tonnage specs contradict each other (2.5 vs 3 ton)? +
Contact the seller immediately for clarification. The 29,000 BTU cooling output and AHRI certificate number (204633301) suggest 2.5-3 ton range. Verify against your home's Manual J load calculation to ensure adequate but not oversized capacity.
