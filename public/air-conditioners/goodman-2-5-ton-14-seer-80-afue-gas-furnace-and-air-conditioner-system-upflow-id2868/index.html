Review

The Goodman 2.5-ton combination gas furnace and air conditioner system positions itself as an ultra-competitive entry-level solution. The upflow A-coil configuration aligns with standard residential applications.

Performance specs reveal a capable mid-range unit with 30,000 BTU cooling capacity delivering 14 SEER efficiency and 100,000 BTU heating at 80% AFUE. The high-efficiency multi-speed blower reaches 2000 CFM maximum airflow, enabling flexible ducting. Single-stage rotary compressor and two-stage gas burner provide adequate cycling without variable capacity sophistication. Frequently Asked Questions

Why does the spec sheet show 3.5-ton tonnage when the product is marketed as 2.5-ton? +
This is a genuine concern. The 3.5-ton rating likely reflects the compressor and condenser capacity, while 2.5-ton targets matched coil size. Oversizing risks short-cycling and efficiency loss. Verify cooling load calculations before installation to ensure proper tonnage matching.
Can I install this myself if I have basic HVAC knowledge? +
Upflow installation is relatively straightforward, but EPA certification requires licensed technicians for refrigerant handling. Ductwork, electrical, and venting you might manage, but refrigerant charging demands proper equipment and credentials to avoid voiding warranty.
How much will this cost to operate annually compared to a 95% AFUE unit? +
An 80% AFUE system wastes 20% of fuel energy versus 5% for 95% AFUE equipment. In cold climates, this creates $300-$600 annual heating cost difference. Payback depends on local gas rates and winter severity, but higher efficiency gains value in heating-dominated regions.
Is 14 SEER competitive with modern air conditioners? +
Fourteen SEER represents solid mid-range cooling efficiency but falls behind current 16-18 SEER standards. For mild and moderate climates, it provides acceptable comfort without premium pricing. Hot climates benefit from higher SEER ratings due to extended cooling seasons.
Does this system qualify for utility rebates or tax credits? +
No. Energy Star certification requirement disqualifies this unit from most federal tax credits and utility rebate programs. These incentives typically target 95%+ AFUE and 16+ SEER equipment, offsetting premium costs.
