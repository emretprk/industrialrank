Review

The Goodman 3.5 Ton Upflow Gas Furnace and Air Conditioner System represents a middle-ground option in the residential HVAC market. Goodman equipment typically appeals to cost-conscious contractors and homeowners seeking reliability without premium branding. This particular configuration bundles a 96% AFUE furnace capable of 80,000 BTU heating output with a 42,000 BTU (3.5-ton) cooling coil. The upflow design suits basements and crawlspaces where horizontal or upflow discharge makes sense for ductwork distribution.

Performance specs here tell a solid efficiency story. The furnace's 96% AFUE rating means 96 cents of every heating dollar converts to home warmth, with minimal stack losses thanks to PVC flue venting. Cooling efficiency registers at 16 SEER, competitive for non-variable-compressor designs. The variable-speed ECM blower motor is the efficiency workhorse, modulating airflow between 0-1600 CFM to match actual heating and cooling demand rather than running full-blast constantly. Two-stage burner heating provides capacity matching that reduces cycling.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does the spec sheet list both 3.5 ton cooling and 5 ton tonnage? +
This is a compatibility notation issue. The actual cooling capacity is 3.5 tons (42,000 BTU), matching the air conditioner coil. The 5-ton reference reflects furnace casing size accommodating larger coils. Install this as a 3.5-ton system to avoid oversizing heating load calculations.
Can this unit handle both natural gas and propane? +
Yes. The two-stage burner accepts either fuel type via simple orifice plate changes during installation. No compressor or electrical modifications needed. Confirm your local fuel supply and ensure proper regulator pressure settings, typically 3.5 inches water column for propane.
How much will the variable-speed blower save on electricity? +
ECM motors use 50-70% less blower energy than fixed-speed models, translating to roughly $100-150 annual savings on blower operation alone. Combined with the high AFUE rating, expect 15-25% total heating cost reduction compared to 14 AFUE systems.
Is 16 SEER good, or should I wait for 18 SEER? +
16 SEER sits solidly above minimum requirements and qualifies for most Energy Star rebates. Higher SEER units typically cost 30-40% more for 10-15% efficiency gains, making payback lengthy in moderate climates. This strikes a practical balance for most homeowners.
What does the 10-year warranty actually cover? +
Compressor, coil, and furnace cabinet parts are covered against manufacturer defects. Refrigerant top-ups, filters, blower motors, and labor are excluded. Register online within 30 days of installation to activate coverage.
