Review

The Goodman 5-ton upflow system sits in a sweet spot for mainstream HVAC replacement projects. This unit combines 100,000 BTU heating capacity with 45,500 BTU cooling into a single all-in-one package, eliminating the need to source separate furnace and coil components. At $117, it's priced competitively for contractors and DIY installers who want proven Goodman reliability without boutique brand markup. The unit qualifies as ENERGY STAR certified, signaling solid efficiency across both heating and cooling modes.

Performance specs tell the real story here. The 96% AFUE rating on the furnace is genuinely impressive for the price point, burning fuel far more efficiently than baseline 80-85% models common in older homes. The air conditioning side delivers 16 SEER1 efficiency, exceeding 2023 regional minimums in most markets. The scroll compressor is field-proven technology, and the variable-speed blower running up to 2000 CFM matches airflow to actual heating or cooling demand. Two-stage burner modulation prevents short-cycling and temperature swings.

Installation

This unit is designed for upflow installation in basements or utility closets with overhead ductwork. It requires 115V, 60Hz single-phase power with breaker sizing between 23.9 and 40 amps, so most homes need only a dedicated circuit rather than full panel upgrades. Liquid and suction line sizing (3/8 inch and 1 1/8 inch respectively) are standard for 5-ton units. The A-coil configuration requires proper drainage, and 50-foot suction line insulation should be installed per Goodman specifications to prevent refrigerant loss.

Warranty & Reliability

Goodman backs the heat exchanger for the product lifetime, a strong protection against catastrophic furnace failure. Parts coverage extends 10 years when you register online, though labor is customer responsibility. This warranty structure is typical for mid-tier equipment and provides reasonable protection for 10-15 year system life expectations. Registration is simple and unlocks the full coverage, so buyers should complete it immediately upon installation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this truly a 4-ton or 5-ton system based on the specs? +
The specs show conflicting tonnage ratings, but the 45,500 BTU cooling capacity and physical dimensions align with a 5-ton unit. The heating output of 100,000 BTU also supports 5-ton classification. Verify with your HVAC contractor before ordering, and cross-reference the AHRI certificate number 7368836 for exact specifications.
Can I install this myself if I'm experienced with HVAC work? +
Yes, the upflow configuration and standard refrigerant lines make it accessible to experienced DIYers. However, you'll need EPA certification for refrigerant handling, proper evacuation equipment to remove moisture, and vacuum pump capability. Have the system pressure tested and nitrogen purged before charging with R410A refrigerant.
How much money will I actually save on heating bills compared to my 80% AFUE furnace? +
A 96% AFUE unit typically saves 16 percentage points of fuel energy versus an 80% AFUE model. On an average $800 annual heating bill, expect roughly $128 in annual savings. Payback occurs in 3-5 years depending on natural gas prices and heating season severity in your region.
How does the 16 SEER1 rating compare to higher-efficiency systems available today? +
16 SEER1 is solid for the price but not cutting-edge. Variable-capacity or inverter-driven systems reach 18-22 SEER1, offering 10-15% better cooling efficiency. Choose this unit for balanced cost-effectiveness, not maximum efficiency. The single-stage compressor does limit part-load efficiency.
What does the lifetime heat exchanger warranty actually cover? +
It covers manufacturing defects causing cracks, corrosion, or failure of the furnace heat exchanger itself. It does not cover labor, refrigerant, or damage from improper installation, acidic refrigerant breakdown, or water contamination. Registration is mandatory to activate this coverage.
