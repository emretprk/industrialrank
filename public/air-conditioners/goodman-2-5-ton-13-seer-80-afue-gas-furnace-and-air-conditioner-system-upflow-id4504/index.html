Review

The Goodman 2.5-ton combo system at $2,705 represents mainstream HVAC equipment designed for straightforward replacement installations. This upflow unit pairs an 80,000 BTU furnace with an air conditioner, making it suitable for most single-family homes in temperate climates. Goodman, a workhorse brand owned by Daikin, emphasizes reliability over cutting-edge features, which explains both the accessible pricing and no-frills component selection.

Heating efficiency comes in at 80% AFUE with a two-stage burner that cycles between high and low fire for better control. Cooling delivers 30,000 BTU at 13 SEER using R410A refrigerant and a reciprocating compressor. The variable-speed blower motor reaches 2,000 CFM maximum and adapts to load conditions, improving runtime efficiency. At 208/230V single-phase 60Hz, it fits standard residential electrical panels with a 16.7 to 25-amp breaker.

Pros & Cons

Pros

  • Two-stage burner on furnace side provides better temperature modulation and comfort compared to single-stage competitors
  • 80,000 BTU heating capacity with 80% AFUE meets most building codes and delivers reliable winter performance
  • Variable-speed blower motor adjusts CFM up to 2,000, improving efficiency and reducing energy waste during partial-load operation
  • 13 SEER rating on 2.5-ton unit balances cooling performance with reasonable operating costs
  • 10-year warranty with online registration provides solid protection for parts and labor coverage

Cons

  • 80% AFUE is entry-level for furnaces, meaning you're losing one-fifth of fuel energy to exhaust compared to 95%+ systems
  • 13 SEER cooling is competent but not exceptional, with newer systems reaching 16-18 SEER at similar price points
  • Single-stage condenser limits cooling efficiency during partial-load operation on mild days
  • Not Energy Star certified, which may affect rebate eligibility in some utility districts

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is this 80% AFUE instead of 95% when high-efficiency furnaces cost only a few hundred dollars more? +
80% AFUE represents the lower efficiency standard for natural gas and propane furnaces. Higher tiers (90-98% AFUE) require condensing heat exchangers and special venting, adding $1,500-3,000. For owners in moderate climates with 15-year payback horizons or shorter stays, the entry-level option makes financial sense.
Can this system handle both upflow and downflow installation? +
The spec sheet lists upflow and downflow configuration capability, but verify with your installer that your specific space allows proper return air ducting. Downflow installation requires ground-level return and above-unit ductwork, which many homes cannot accommodate without extensive modifications.
How does 13 SEER compare to modern air conditioners? +
13 SEER is respectable but not premium. Current mid-tier units hit 15-16 SEER, and high-end systems reach 18-21 SEER. The difference translates to roughly 15-30% higher cooling costs over 10 years, depending on climate and usage patterns. For mild summers, 13 SEER remains acceptable.
Is Goodman as reliable as Carrier, Trane, or Lennox? +
Goodman compressors and components rank among industry averages with reasonable failure rates. However, Carrier and Trane command premium pricing partly due to brand perception and local dealer support networks. Goodman systems perform well when properly installed and maintained by qualified technicians.
What does the warranty actually cover if something breaks? +
The 10-year coverage includes parts and labor for manufacturing defects. Compressor failure, refrigerant leaks, and coil cracks are covered. Wear items like capacitors and contactors may have shorter timelines. Always review the complete warranty document before purchase.
