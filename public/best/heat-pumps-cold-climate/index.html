How to Choose

The NEEP cold-climate specification: what it actually means

The Northeast Energy Efficiency Partnerships (NEEP) defines a cold-climate heat pump as one achieving at least 70% of rated heating capacity at 5°F ambient. All four units on this list exceed that specification. Standard heat pumps not on this list typically maintain 40–60% capacity at 5°F — still functional, but insufficient as a sole heat source in extended cold periods.

Supplemental heat: when it is and is not needed

A correctly sized cold-climate heat pump does not need supplemental electric resistance heat in Zone 5 and Zone 6 for most winter days. It will run at high output continuously during the coldest 1–5% of winter hours. If the design load calculation shows the heat pump is undersized for the 99% design temperature, supplemental heat is appropriate — but this should be the exception, not the standard installation practice.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a cold-climate heat pump?
A cold-climate heat pump is one that meets the NEEP specification — maintaining at least 70% of rated heating capacity at 5°F ambient. Standard heat pumps do not meet this threshold. Cold-climate units use Enhanced Vapor Injection (EVI) or Flash Injection compressor technology to maintain efficiency at sub-freezing temperatures.
Can a heat pump heat my home in -20°F weather?
Some heat pumps — specifically Daikin Aurora and Mitsubishi Hyper-Heat models — are rated for operation to -22°F and -13°F respectively. At those temperatures, output is reduced but the units continue functioning. In sustained periods below -15°F, supplemental electric resistance or a dual-fuel backup (gas furnace) may be appropriate for the coldest regions.
Is a heat pump cheaper to operate than gas heat in cold climates?
At electricity prices below $0.15/kWh, a cold-climate heat pump is less expensive to operate than a gas furnace in most US climate zones including Zone 5 and Zone 6. Above $0.20/kWh, gas furnaces are typically more economical in very cold climates. The IRA Section 25C tax credit improves the economic case by reducing first cost.
