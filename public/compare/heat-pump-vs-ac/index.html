Heat Pump vs AC

A heat pump does everything an air conditioner does — plus heating. In 2026, the cost difference is smaller than most buyers assume.

Updated June 2026IndustrialRank Editorial
Key Findings
  • Heat pumps cool and heat — ACs cool only
  • At equivalent SEER2, heat pump and AC cooling efficiency are identical
  • IRA Section 25C: 30% tax credit on heat pumps, not on AC-only systems
  • Heat pump installed cost premium over AC: approximately $500–$1,500
  • In zones 3–5, a heat pump replaces both AC and gas/electric heat in one system

IndustrialRank Verdict: Heat Pump Wins for Most New Installations

For any homeowner considering a new cooling system who also has a heating need (virtually all US climates), a heat pump is the better investment at current pricing. The IRA 30% tax credit on heat pumps and the absence of an equivalent AC credit, combined with the heat pump's dual-function capability, make the $500–$1,500 premium over a comparable AC difficult to justify against buying AC alone. The case for AC-only is limited to: homes with a gas furnace already in place where replacing only the AC at lowest cost is the goal, or very mild climates where heating demand is minimal.

Strengths Comparison

Heat Pump Strengths

  • Provides heating — eliminates separate heating system cost
  • IRA 30% tax credit applies
  • Same cooling efficiency as AC at same SEER2
  • One system, one service call, one warranty

Air Conditioner (AC) Strengths

  • Lower equipment cost if heating already covered by gas
  • Higher SEER2 available at premium tier (26 SEER2 vs 22 for heat pumps)
  • Simpler system in mild climates where heating is rarely needed
  • Lower upfront cost if heating system is recently replaced

Analysis

A heat pump is mechanically identical to a central air conditioner with one addition: a reversing valve that allows the refrigerant to run in both directions. In cooling mode, a heat pump is an air conditioner — extracting heat from inside and releasing it outside. In heating mode, the reversing valve switches direction: the system extracts heat from outside air and releases it inside. The cooling efficiency (SEER2) of a heat pump and an air conditioner at the same specifications is identical.

The IRA Section 25C tax credit fundamentally changed the heat pump vs AC value equation. A homeowner who installs a $6,000 heat pump system qualifies for a $1,800 tax credit (30% of $6,000). The same homeowner who installs a $5,000 air conditioner (commonly the cheaper alternative) gets no federal credit. After the credit, the heat pump at $4,200 net cost vs the AC at $5,000 is actually cheaper — before accounting for the eliminated heating system cost.

The case for buying an AC alone in 2026 is limited. It applies most clearly when: (1) a gas furnace was recently replaced and is in good condition, making the incremental cost of a heat pump unjustified against a new AC replacement cost alone; (2) the home is in a very mild climate where heating demand is minimal and the heat pump's heating capability provides negligible value; or (3) the homeowner specifically needs a higher SEER2 than available heat pump models provide for cooling-intensive applications.

For new construction, the AC vs heat pump decision is clear: install the heat pump. New construction has no existing heating system to preserve. The heat pump eliminates the need for a separate furnace or boiler installation, the IRA credit reduces net cost, and the operational efficiency of heat pump heating (COP 3.0 vs electric resistance COP 1.0) reduces lifetime operating cost substantially.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a heat pump the same as an air conditioner?
A heat pump and an air conditioner are mechanically similar — both use refrigerant to move heat. The key difference: a heat pump includes a reversing valve that allows it to operate in heating mode (extracting heat from outdoor air and delivering it inside) as well as cooling mode. An air conditioner can only cool. In cooling mode, a heat pump and an air conditioner with the same SEER2 rating have identical energy efficiency.
Does a heat pump cool as well as an air conditioner?
Yes. At equivalent SEER2 ratings, a heat pump and an air conditioner provide identical cooling performance. The reversing valve adds to the heat pump's cost but does not affect cooling efficiency. A heat pump at 18 SEER2 cools as effectively as an air conditioner at 18 SEER2.
What is the IRA tax credit for heat pumps vs air conditioners?
The IRA Section 25C residential energy efficiency credit provides 30% of qualifying heat pump installation costs, up to $2,000 per household per year through 2032. Air conditioner replacements do not qualify for an equivalent federal credit. This effectively subsidizes heat pump installation by $600–$2,000 vs AC installation, changing the economics of the decision for most buyers.
Can a heat pump replace both my AC and furnace?
Yes. A ducted central heat pump with an air handler replaces both the central air conditioner and the furnace. In heating mode, it operates similarly to a furnace but without combustion. Cold-climate heat pumps (Mitsubishi, Bosch IDS, Carrier Infinity) can serve as the primary or sole heat source in zones 3–5 without supplemental resistance heat. In zones 6–7 with extreme cold, electric resistance backup strips in the air handler activate when outdoor temperature exceeds the heat pump's operating floor.
Is a heat pump more expensive than an air conditioner?
At purchase, yes — typically $500–$1,500 more than an equivalent central AC. After the IRA 30% tax credit, the net cost difference narrows to $0–$500 in many scenarios. Over the system's lifetime, a heat pump eliminates the need for a separate heating system — the total lifecycle cost of a heat pump system vs an AC + furnace combination typically favors the heat pump in zones 3–5.
