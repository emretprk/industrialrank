Tankless vs Tank Water Heater

Tankless water heaters deliver hot water on demand and never run out — but cost more upfront. Is the efficiency savings worth it?

Updated June 2026IndustrialRank Editorial
Key Findings
  • Tankless: 24–34% more efficient than tank heaters for average household use
  • Tankless lifespan: 20+ years vs tank heater 10–15 years
  • Tankless upfront cost: $1,200–$3,500 installed vs $500–$1,500 for tank
  • IRA Section 25C: $600 credit on qualifying heat pump water heaters
  • Tankless best for: smaller households, low/moderate hot water demand, space-constrained installs

IndustrialRank Verdict: Tankless Wins on Lifetime Value; Tank Wins on Upfront Cost

Tankless wins on efficiency (no standby heat loss), lifespan (20+ yr vs 10–15 yr), and infinite hot water supply. Tank wins on upfront cost and whole-house high-demand performance when multiple hot water draws happen simultaneously. For most households, tankless provides better lifetime value. For large households with simultaneous high-demand use (multiple showers, dishwasher, laundry at same time), a tank water heater sized for the load or a heat pump water heater may be the better choice.

Strengths Comparison

Tankless Water Heater Strengths

  • No standby heat loss — energy only used when hot water flows
  • Unlimited hot water — never runs out
  • 20+ year lifespan vs 10–15 for tank
  • Space-saving wall mount — no floor footprint
  • Lower lifetime operating cost

Tank Water Heater Strengths

  • Lower upfront installed cost
  • Handles simultaneous high-demand draws from tank buffer
  • Simpler installation — no gas pressure/flow requirements
  • Standard service available from any plumber
  • Heat pump water heater option provides excellent efficiency at lower cost than tankless

Analysis

The standby heat loss difference is the core efficiency advantage of tankless water heaters. A tank water heater maintains 40–80 gallons of hot water continuously — if no hot water is being used, the heater still fires periodically to maintain setpoint temperature. The US DOE estimates standby losses account for 20–30% of total water heating energy in typical households. A tankless heater consumes energy only when hot water is flowing — no standby loss.

For households with low to moderate hot water demand — a couple, a small family, or a household with spread-out hot water use across the day — tankless is clearly more efficient. Rinnai and Navien are the market leaders for residential gas tankless units; Stiebel Eltron and Bosch lead the electric tankless segment. Properly sized tankless units achieve EF ratings of 0.82–0.96 vs typical tank heater EF of 0.58–0.70.

The limitation of tankless heaters is simultaneous demand. A single tankless unit rated for 5–7 gallons per minute (standard for residential) handles one or two simultaneous draws comfortably. A household running two showers, a dishwasher, and laundry simultaneously can exceed a single tankless unit's flow rate — resulting in temperature drop. For large households with high simultaneous demand, either a larger tankless unit, two units in parallel, or a well-sized tank heater is more appropriate.

Heat pump water heaters deserve mention in this comparison. At $1,000–$1,800 installed and EF ratings of 3.0–4.0 (COP), heat pump water heaters are significantly more efficient than both gas tankless and tank heaters at current electricity rates. The IRA Section 25C provides a $600 credit on qualifying heat pump water heaters. For households in zones 1–4 where a heat pump water heater is practical (requires space in conditioned or semi-conditioned area), they often provide better lifecycle value than gas tankless.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a tankless water heater worth it?
For most households, yes — over a 15–20 year ownership period. Annual operating savings of $80–$150 vs a standard tank heater, combined with a 20+ year lifespan vs 10–15 years for tanks, typically recover the $700–$2,000 installed cost premium within 8–15 years. The value case is strongest for smaller households with lower simultaneous hot water demand.
What is the best tankless water heater brand?
Rinnai and Navien are the leading gas residential tankless brands in the US market. Rinnai has the longer track record and wider service network. Navien's condensing models achieve higher efficiency ratings. For electric tankless, Stiebel Eltron and Bosch are the established quality brands. Cheaper brands (Rinnai knock-offs) have inconsistent reliability records.
Does a tankless water heater ever run out of hot water?
No. A tankless heater heats water continuously on demand — there is no tank to deplete. As long as the flow rate of hot water demand does not exceed the unit's rated GPM capacity, you will not run out of hot water. Exceeding the unit's GPM capacity results in temperature drop, not a complete loss of hot water.
Can a tankless water heater serve a whole house?
Yes. A properly sized whole-house gas tankless unit (7–10 GPM) serves most households. Sizing should account for peak simultaneous demand — how many hot water draws will be occurring at the same time. Rinnai's RU199IN and similar large-capacity units handle whole-house demand in most residential configurations.
What is the IRA credit for water heaters?
Heat pump water heaters qualify for the IRA Section 25C residential energy efficiency credit — $600 per household per year (not a percentage; a fixed $600 for water heaters). Standard gas or electric tank and tankless heaters do not qualify for an equivalent federal credit. The $600 credit applies to the product and installation cost combined.
