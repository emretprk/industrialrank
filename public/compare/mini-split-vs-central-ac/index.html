Mini Split vs Central AC

Mini splits and central AC serve different applications. The right choice depends on your ductwork, home size, budget, and whether you need zone control.

Updated June 2026IndustrialRank Editorial
Key Findings
  • Mini splits: no ductwork required — 15–30% lower energy loss vs central AC
  • Central AC: lower upfront cost for whole-home cooling if ductwork already exists
  • Mini splits: zone control — condition only occupied rooms
  • Mini splits: higher SEER2 ratings available (up to 21) vs central AC (up to 24 at premium)
  • Central AC: single unit for whole home — simpler operation and maintenance

IndustrialRank Verdict: Mini Split Wins for New Installations and Zone Control

For homes without existing ductwork, additions, garages, and applications requiring zone control, mini splits are the correct choice. For whole-home cooling in homes with existing quality ductwork and no zone control requirement, central AC is typically lower total cost. Mini splits' duct-free advantage (15–30% efficiency improvement over ducted systems) makes them competitive even against high-SEER central AC in homes where ductwork is in poor condition or in unconditioned attics.

  • No duct energy losses — 15–30% efficiency advantage

Central AC Strengths

  • Standard contractor install — wide service availability
  • Easier to integrate with whole-home air filtration and humidity control

Analysis

The mini split vs central AC decision is fundamentally about ductwork and application. In a home with existing quality ductwork in conditioned space, central AC is usually the lower total cost option for whole-home cooling — the ductwork investment has already been made and a single outdoor unit serves all rooms. In a home without ductwork, a home addition, a garage, or a room that is either not connected to the central system or poorly served by it, a mini split is the cost-effective and energy-efficient solution.

Duct losses are the frequently overlooked efficiency factor. US homes with ducts in unconditioned attics lose 15–30% of conditioned air to duct leakage and conduction before it reaches the living space. A central AC system rated 16 SEER2 with 20% duct loss has an effective delivered efficiency of approximately 12.8 SEER2. A mini split rated 19 SEER2 delivering conditioned air directly into the room has no equivalent loss — making it more energy efficient in real-world operation even if its nameplate SEER2 is lower.

Zone control is the operational advantage that most influences mini split satisfaction. Central AC with a single thermostat conditions the entire home to the same setpoint regardless of occupancy. A mini split system with one unit per room allows each room to be conditioned independently — running the bedroom at 68°F while the unoccupied living room is at 78°F saves meaningful energy in part-time occupancy applications.

For a homeowner comparing a 3-zone mini split system (bedroom, living room, home office) against a central AC upgrade, the mini split total cost ($4,500–$12,000 for 3 zones installed) is often comparable to or lower than a central AC replacement with duct improvements ($5,000–$15,000). At equivalent total cost, the mini split delivers better zone control, heat pump capability, and no duct loss penalty.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a mini split cool a whole house?
Yes. A multi-zone mini split system with one indoor unit per room can condition an entire home. System capacity scales from 18,000 to 54,000+ BTU for whole-home applications. The trade-off vs central AC: multiple indoor wall units are visible in each room, installation cost is typically higher per BTU, and multi-zone systems require careful zone load balancing to maintain efficiency.
Are mini splits more expensive than central AC?
Compared system-to-system, mini splits are more expensive per BTU than central AC in homes with existing ductwork. In homes without ductwork, mini splits are significantly less expensive than installing new ductwork plus central AC. Zone-controlled mini split systems can be comparable in total installed cost to central AC with add-on zoning systems.
What is the SEER2 rating of the best mini splits?
The highest SEER2-rated mini splits as of 2026 are the Mitsubishi H2i+ at 21.0 and Bosch IDS Premium at 20.2. In the central AC category, the Lennox XC25 achieves 26 SEER2 — but this is only relevant with fully sealed ductwork in conditioned space. In real-world installations with duct losses, effective delivered efficiency is lower.
Do mini splits need ductwork?
No. Mini split systems use refrigerant lines (linesets) running between the outdoor unit and indoor wall units — no ductwork required. The indoor unit delivers conditioned air directly into the room. This is the fundamental advantage over central AC in homes without existing ductwork.
Can a mini split heat and cool?
Yes. Mini splits are heat pumps — they can both heat and cool by reversing refrigerant flow direction. In heating mode, a mini split extracts heat from outdoor air and delivers it inside. Cold-climate models (Mitsubishi Hyper-Heat) maintain heating capability to -13°F. This means a single mini split installation replaces both the air conditioner and the heating system.
