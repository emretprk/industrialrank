Heat Pump vs Gas Furnace

The most important HVAC buying decision in 2026 — with IRA tax credits, rising gas prices, and heat pump technology improvements changing the calculus in most US climate zones.

IndustrialRank Verdict: Heat Pump Wins in Most Climates

In climate zones 1–5, a heat pump operating at COP 2.5–3.5 outperforms a gas furnace on lifecycle energy cost at current electricity and natural gas prices in most US markets. The IRA Section 25C 30% tax credit on heat pumps and the absence of equivalent gas furnace incentives widen the financial advantage. In zone 6–7 with extreme cold, a cold-climate heat pump (Mitsubishi Hyper-Heat, Bosch IDS Premium) combined with backup resistance remains competitive with gas. The gas furnace advantage narrows to very cold climates, homes already on gas with existing furnace infrastructure, and regions with very low gas prices and high electricity rates.

Analysis

The heat pump vs gas furnace decision in 2026 is substantially different from the same comparison in 2020. Three factors have shifted the calculus toward heat pumps: the IRA Section 25C 30% tax credit (effective 2023, running through 2032), the introduction of Hyper-Heat cold-climate mini splits that operate at -13°F to -22°F, and natural gas price increases of 20–30% in many US markets since 2020.

Efficiency comparison requires care. Gas furnace AFUE ratings of 95–98% mean 95–98 cents of heat per dollar of gas. A heat pump at COP 3.0 produces 300 cents of heat per dollar of electricity — three times the efficiency. The relevant comparison is operating cost, which depends on local electricity and gas prices. At national average rates ($0.15/kWh electricity, $1.30/therm gas), a heat pump is 20–40% cheaper to operate for heating in zones 3–5.

Cold climate performance was the historical barrier to heat pump adoption in zones 5–7. Standard heat pumps lose 30–50% of heating capacity at 5°F. Mitsubishi's Hyper-Heat GL series maintains 100% rated capacity at 5°F and operates to -13°F. The MRCOOL Oasis and Bosch IDS Premium add additional cold-climate options. In zones 5–6, a cold-climate heat pump with backup electric resistance (available as the electric heat strip in most air handlers) is now the technically and economically competitive alternative to gas in most scenarios.

The case for gas furnace is strongest in: (1) zones 6–7 where extreme cold is frequent and backup heat costs are high, (2) homes with existing gas infrastructure where the furnace-only replacement cost is significantly lower than a full heat pump system, and (3) markets with very low gas prices and high electricity rates. Buyers in California, New York, and Massachusetts — where electricity rates are high but gas rates are also high and utility rebates for heat pumps are available — should model the specific numbers before deciding.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a heat pump cheaper to run than a gas furnace?
In most US climate zones at national average energy prices, yes. A heat pump at COP 3.0 produces 3 units of heat per unit of electricity. At $0.15/kWh electricity and $1.30/therm gas, the heat pump operating cost is approximately 25–35% lower for equivalent heat output. The comparison shifts against the heat pump in markets with very low gas prices (parts of the South, Texas) or very high electricity rates (Hawaii, some California utilities).
Can a heat pump replace a gas furnace in a cold climate?
Yes, if you select a cold-climate rated model. Mitsubishi Hyper-Heat, MRCOOL Oasis H2i+, and Bosch IDS Premium all operate to -13°F to -22°F minimum with meaningful heat output at these temperatures. Standard heat pumps (minimum 5°F to 0°F) require supplemental electric resistance heat in cold climates. Cold-climate heat pumps in zones 5–6 typically serve as primary heat without supplemental resistance on all but the coldest 5–10% of winter hours.
What is the IRA tax credit for heat pumps?
The Inflation Reduction Act Section 25C residential energy efficiency credit provides 30% of qualifying heat pump installation costs, up to $2,000 per household per year through 2032. Gas furnace replacements do not qualify for an equivalent credit. At a $6,000 installed heat pump system, the credit is $1,800 — reducing net cost to $4,200. Stacked with available utility rebates, net installed cost can be reduced further.
What is a good heat pump for cold climates?
The Mitsubishi MSZ-GL series (19 SEER2, -13°F minimum, 12-year warranty) is the benchmark for cold-climate mini splits. The Mitsubishi H2i+ (21 SEER2, -22°F operation) is the premium option. For central ducted systems, the Carrier Infinity 20 and Lennox XP21 operate to -4°F — adequate for zones 4–5. In zone 6–7 with ducted systems, supplemental electric resistance backup is recommended.
Do I need backup heat with a heat pump?
In climate zones 1–4, a properly sized cold-climate heat pump generally does not need backup heat. In zones 5–6 with a cold-climate mini split (Mitsubishi GL, Bosch IDS), backup heat is needed for only the coldest 5–10% of winter hours. Central heat pump systems in zones 5–7 are typically specified with electric resistance backup strips in the air handler — these activate automatically when outdoor temperature exceeds the heat pump's operating floor.
